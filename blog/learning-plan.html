A description of your own strengths and limitations in relation to learning

Well let’s start off with the positives, some strengths.

If I’m really enjoying a particularly subject, challenge or working towards a goal I truly value, I tend to absorb information fairly well. I genuinely have a passion for learning as much as possible about just about any subject of interest, and having a true passion for your day to day work is about the best way to make sure it’s a success!

I’m also very resilient and persistent. When problems come up, big or small, which they always tend to do, I tend not to get stressed out or panic or walk away from the issue. For me, each potential setback is just another step that has to be taken to get to the endpoint, which I’m hoping will work well in the development world where the problems are endless but the solutions are oh so satisfying.

On to the limitations.

Firstly, my attention span. I’ve mentioned it a couple times here and there but I have ADHD and have done my whole life, although I only received an official diagnosis a few months before coming to EDA (in fact, waiting for the diagnosis is part of why I waited to enroll).

As far as limitations go, an actual learning-related disorder is a big one but one that is being very well managed and it’s something that I’ve made huge progress on in the past few months, which is a big source of pride for me.

It still comes up every now and then, but right now it’s very much under control and so far hasn’t interfered with me working through sprints 1 and 2, but it’s something I do have to be aware of just in case.

I also struggle to actually ask for help. A good friend (and former manager) of mine and I had a chat about this and we basically decided that everyone sits somewhere on a scale from Asker to Googler. 

Some people come across something they don’t understand and their first instinct is to ask someone else what it means, escalating up levels of experience/knowledge/seniority until they get the information.

Others will turn to Google and escalate through various searches to find their answer.

Where you sit on the spectrum basically comes down to how long it takes you to give up on searching yourself and raise your hand to ask someone else.

Our conclusion was that I sit about as far to the Google side of the spectrum as possible, which can be good in that I tend to learn a lot more that way and it means I solve problems myself rather than roping in others who are probably focusing on their own work, but my reluctance to ask for help definitely can lead to me spending way too long down a rabbit hole trying to solve a problem that the person 2 metres to my left already knows the answer to.

A commitment to workload management

First, how will I manage myself:

I tend to be very output or goal oriented. My method throughout these first sprints has been to check the learning competencies and timebox for an idea of how to work through the section, then work through the tasks one by one until they’re completed.My approach is always to break down any large segment into individual tasks that I need to perform, then order them in a logical way and work down the list.

Working in a team, I tend to do things in a similar way. Overall, the team has a goal and to achieve that goal certain criteria needs to be met. If each person is responsible for a set of criteria and understands the tasks required to fulfill them, I’ve found it’s been pretty straightforward to know where we’re all at, how we’re tracking, which tasks are dependent on others and so on. Hasn’t failed me yet!

When it comes to things building up and getting hectic:

I like to think I do well under pressure. I’ve been in stressful situations plenty of times, where tasks are piling up under tight deadlines and I think there are a few aspects to getting through those times.Firstly, prioritising tasks, understanding which are totally non-negotiable, which can be reduced, which can be shifted back and so on, is very useful. If I’ve got 8 hours of work and only 5 hours to do it, there’s likely a few things that can have less time spent if they’re not critical, or can be done to a sort of MVP level and enhanced later or can have their deadlines extended.In those situations, communicating with anyone who is requiring my outputs is my next move, get those expectations set ASAP so they’re not expecting a result when one isn’t coming.If that’s not working, it’s usually time to stand up, get out of the office/room/building, switch off the tech and clear my head. That might just be going for a walk, grabbing a coffee or whatever. That’s usually where discussing it with others comes in, because they can either help directly with the problem, or at least act as a wall to bounce off - providing they’re willing, of course!

How and where I’ll seek help

Another logical, maths-y answer here. If, say, I have a timebox saying that 4 tasks should be taking me 2 hours and I’ve passed the half hour mark and aren’t close to getting the first one done, I’m struggling and it’s time to change the approach. If I’ve gotten to that point, my own problem solving likely isn’t giving me the solution so that’s where others come in.My rough path of escalation would be to start with my teammates, whether that’s asking in slack (either foundations, cohort or code-help-desk) or asking in person if we’re all together. In the past, that solved the vast majority of problems already.

If my teammates don’t have the answer, or can’t figure it out without impacting their work, then we’re going up the ladder to EDA staff. That’ll be whoever is the facilitator I’m working with in foundations or bootcamp. This is also where I’d go for more ‘meta’ questions; is this the correct format for submission, am I working through this content in the right way, and so on, since my cohort is learning it at the same time I am!

What I expect from the facilitation team

A few things, obviously being available to answer burning questions is important, within reason. I’m fine with a 24 hour response time, really. People have their own work to be getting on with!

Patience is also important. I haven’t hit any massive roadblocks just yet, but this is only the end of Sprint 2 so things are going to get a lot tougher and I’m sure there’ll be times where I can’t quite wrap my head around a concept on the first try!

Lastly, mental health is going to matter. I mentioned I do well with stress, but when it does get rough I have a tendency to avoid asking for help for longer than I really should, so having a facilitation team that understands the struggles likely to come (and I’m sure they’re pretty experienced at this!) is going to be a huge help.

Any scheduling information?

Absolutely nothing! The only rigidly scheduled part of my daily routine was my job, which I left to join EDA, so feel free to absorb as much of my time as you like! A little time off on weekends would be nice, of course, but I’m pretty comfortable making this the centre of my world for the foreseeable future!
