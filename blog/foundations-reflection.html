Hasn’t presented any kind of issue so far, but definitely something I’d noticed and something I’ve never been able to do before - normally I’ve been the exact opposite, my whole day is a series of breaks with brief moments of work in between. How do values, empathy and self awareness working with learning and programming?

So many ways!

Let’s start with learning

Learning is influenced by all of these to a massive degree. 

Your values are hugely important, if you value frank discussions, self directed tasks and freedom to go at your own pace then that’s how you’ll learn best. For example, I know I value structure, clear expectations and the ability to get stuck in a try stuff until it works - so I know that while watching an intro video is a nice warmup, I need to focus on the practical tasks (blogs, coding, exercises) to make the most of my learning.Self awareness is probably the greatest tool when it comes to learning. The more you understand your strengths and limitations, as well as how you react to things, manage yourself and your feelings and how your brain works, the better you can approach learning the right way.

I’m constitutionally incapable of self-monitoring, so these reflections, blog posts and discussions are extremely important for getting me to recognise and take in what I’ve been learning and feeling. I also tend to switch off if I have too much free time or lack of structure in a task or day, so I know that finding external sources of structure when they aren’t built into my day’s tasks is vital to keep me focused and productive - having people that know what I want to accomplish in a day and will check in on how it went at the end of the day as well as very clear expectations of which small tasks need doing and when is extremely useful in my day, otherwise I can pretty easily spend several hours writing a blog post that only needed to be a quick discussion.

Lastly, empathy is huge. First for your peers; taking the time and energy to check in with the people around you, understand how they’re feeling and what they might be struggling with let’s you connect better with them (huge when you’re in a team) and support each other, but it also reminds you that the things you’re struggling with are common struggles across the whole team - you’re not alone in the struggle, you’re not struggling because you can’t do it, it’s just hard and everyone is in the same boat!

How about programming?

Programming is influenced in very similar ways to learning, in fact programming is a permanent state of learning so maybe that shouldn’t be surprising!

Values matter here in a big way. When we’re coding, we’re creating something. Something that could be used by other people, worked on by others or directly affect them. Sticking to your values and making sure that what you make lives up to your values is important to working with pride in your creations.

Empathy is probably the biggest, for me. When it comes to programming out in the wild, I’ll be working in a team, writing code that others will have to work with and creating software that others will use. This ties in with self awareness too, being aware of how you program is vital in assessing the impact it’ll have on others.I know that my code can get a little hard to follow at times, a product of my tendency to take great leaps in logic, jump between functions and take sometimes confusing routes to completing tasks (thanks brain). That’s fine by me, I wrote the code so I generally know how it works, but it can be tough to follow if you’re just picking it up and even worse if you’re trying to follow along as I write it (along with my difficulty self-monitoring, explaining my thoughts is extremely difficult).

That means I need to be sure my variables are very clearly labelled, I’ve got the right comments in there to keep it clear and I can explain how it works to people.

Empathy for the users and customers is also important. If your creation is intended to be used by people in stressful circumstances (medical or financial software, for example) then it needs to be as simple, clear and calming as possible. If someone is trying to use an app to call roadside assistance after a car crash, sending them through a half dozen submenus, information fields and logins is definitely not going to make life easy for them.

So what’s been surprising? What was tough?

I definitely surprised myself with the growth mindset and neuroplasticity sections. They’re both concepts I’d had an awareness of before, but I’d never actually done any proper research or learning. 

What I felt wasn’t surprise, but the exact opposite!

It turns out I’d always operated under those two principles! It wasn’t conscious, I didn’t have a label or background for it, but they’re both things I’ve generally believed to be true in general. You can always learn things, otherwise you’d never be able to drive to a new place, spell a new word, find a new favourite lunch spot or remember a phone number as an adult - those things are all learning as much as understanding how to code is - and it makes sense that you can get better with practice.

In terms of difficulty, the hardest task to actually do were the listening and looping. I’ve mentioned my ADHD a handful of times here, but this is a particularly relevant topic. It’s not something that bothers me or I try to hide, with the right things in place it generally isn’t a hugely negative part of me (sometimes positive!) - but tasks like this, or that first question here, are almost perfectly incompatible with my brain!

It took a bit of practice to get those right, but being aware of how I react to listening and conversations and taking the right steps to prepare for them means I can manage without too much trouble (at least in small doses!).

What’s the importance of all this core stuff anyway?

Ahhh.. I have a bit of insight into this, given my previous job was entirely about placing people into jobs in the tech world - especially developers.Assuming someone had the experience and skills to land an interview, the overwhelming reason they got a job offer or not, the biggest deciding factor, was their interpersonal and core skills. I don’t mean having developed strong core skills gave them an edge over others, I mean it was 100% make or break.

Working in this field means working with others, constantly dealing with relationships, politics, requests, demands, roadblocks and complications - without the core skills to overcome challenges or work effectively with other people, it’s extremely difficult to work successfully and THAT is what employers always look for.

Core skills don’t just make getting through the learning journey easier, they make you more successful in your coding and your career.

So is the core learning valuable?

... Did you read that last section? Of course it’s valuable! 

Programming is a tool, it lets you make things that solve a problem. But without the core skills, it’s hard to actually use those tools to make the right thing, to make it with others and to use the tools to your best potential.

Imagine being a builder. You can be as good as you like at joining together bits of wood, making fancy windows and all the rest. You know how to programme the crap out of a house!

But without developing any core skills, you’ll find it harder to deal with your customers, the other tradespeople and you’ll struggle to understand what your customers really want built and how to work with them.Basically, the programming is a tool but the core skills let you use it to a much greater potential!
